6  Chemical reactions and microbiology

Chemical reactions affect the quantity of stuff…

6.1 Stoichiometry

Chemical stoichiometry just means the coefficients that accompany the reactants and products in a balanced chemical reaction. For example, the simple organic compound urea (a waste product in mammal urine) is hydrolyzed like this:

\[ \ce{CO(NH2)2 + H2O -> 2NH3 + CO2} \tag{6.1}\]

So the stoichiometry shows that hydrolysis of one mole of urea consumes one mole of water and produces two moles of ammonia and one of carbon dioxide. Why do we care? Well, the stoichiometric coefficients can then be used directly in a model to get state variable derivatives from the reaction rate, which itself is calculated using the appropriate constitutive equation. So in the framework presented in Chapter 2, the stoichiometric coefficients go into creating linking equations (Section 2.5). We then just need a single constitutive equation for the overall reaction rate (we will get to that in the next section) and simple linking equations for each chemical species. For example, if our reaction rate is \(r\), with units of \(\molpmcps\), then the derivative for \(\ce{CO(NH2)2}\) would be

\[ \frac{dc_{urea}}{dt} = -1 \cdot r, \]

and for ammonia,

\[ \frac{dc_{\ce{NH3}}}{dt} = 2 \cdot r. \]

The coefficients come directly from Equation 6.1. Amazingly simple, isn’t it? When implemented in Python (and written, for that matter) a coefficient array can be combined with a state variable array for compact and efficient code when there is more than one state variable, as implied in the above example. We will see some of that later.

There are some complications that come from other units. In many cases, it is not moles but mass, or even mass of a particular element that is of interest; then the coefficients need to be adjusted.

6.2 Reaction rates

The constitutive equation for a chemical reaction must quantify its rate, or how fast it proceeds, e.g., \(\molps\), \(\molpmcps\), or on a mass basis, e.g., \(\gpmcps\). The term kinetics is used to describe reaction rates. Here we will look at two simple cases: first-order reactions and second-order reactions.

A reaction that follows first-order kinetics has a rate that is proportional to the quantity of reactant present. So:

\[ r_m = \frac{dM}{dt} = - k \cdot M, \tag{6.2}\]

for mass or

\[ r_c = \frac{dc}{dt} = - k \cdot c, \tag{6.3}\]

for concentration. The \(k\) is the first-order reaction rate constant with dimensions \(\text{T}^{-1}\). It is not necessarily constant, and depends at least on temperature for most chemical reactions. But its value does not change as the reaction proceeds, at least not for a true first-order reaction. A classic example of a first-order reaction is radioactive decay. Many other chemical reactions are not truly first-order, but come close for modelling purposes.

The rate of a simple first-order reaction can be described analytically with the exponential model (see Section 3.3). We’ll show that here. Compare Equation 6.3 to Equation 3.1. What specific variables or parameters go in for the \(x\) and \(\lambda\) in Equation 3.1? Well, \(x = c\) and \(\lambda = -k\). Note the minus sign! Now take a look at Equation 3.2 and plug these in. That gives:

\[ c = c_0 \cdot e^{-k \cdot t}. \tag{6.4}\]

Simple!

With this simple analytical model we can predict the concentration of our reactant (perhaps urea) at any time. We could also predict the rate by combining Equation 6.4 and Equation 3.1.

If a reaction includes two reactants it might be possible to approximate the observed rate with a second-order model, i.e.,

\[ r_c = \frac{dc}{dt} = - k \cdot c_1 \cdot c_2, \tag{6.5}\]

where \(c_1\) is the concentration of the first reactant and \(c_2\) the second one. While there are analytical solutions for some second-order reaction scenarios, we won’t cover them (you can read more here). But Equation 6.5 can be dropped into a numerical model easily!

6.3 Equilibrium reactions

Rapid reversible chemical reactions are often at equilibrium, meaning the concentrations of chemical species do not change over time. Here we will look at one type of equibrium reaction: acid-base or proton exchange. For more on these types of problems, see Snoeyink (1980).

Acid-base reactions are important in natural and engineered systems for influencing pH, which affects many processes. In both types of systems, a set of chemical species and reactions that are together called the carbonate system are an important system. The carbonate system plays an important role in processes in the oceans, municipal wastewater, and even your blood! Several chemical species play a role:

  • \(\ce{CO2(g)}\) is a gas,
  • \(\ce{CO2(aq)}\) is a what you get when \(\ce{CO2}\) dissolves in water–it is an uncharged solute surrounded by water molecules,
  • \(\ce{H2CO3}\) is called carbonic acid, and is formed when \(\ce{CO2(aq)}\) reacts with water in a hydration reaction,
  • \(\ce{H2CO3*}\) is not actually a chemical species, but the sum of \(\ce{CO2(aq)}\) and \(\ce{H2CO3}\), used because it is difficult to measure the two separately,
  • \(\ce{HCO3-}\) is bicarbonate, and is the most important buffer component in some systems, and
  • \(\ce{CO32-}\) is carbonate.

Chemical speciation models are used to determine the concentration of specific chemical species. Why might this matter? Well, chemical species affect biological and chemical processes.

The fundamental concept here is that the mass action expression or reaction quotient takes on a specific numeric value at equilibrium. For example, let’s look at a reaction between only two of the carbonate system species:

\[ \ce{H2CO3^* <=> HCO3- + H+}. \tag{6.6}\]

The mass action expression or reaction quotient for any reaction is the (mathematical) product of (reaction) product concentrations divided by the product of reactant concentrations. Each concentration should be raised to the power of the stoichiometric coefficient (all 1 in this reaction). So for Equation 6.6, we have:

\[ \frac{c_{\ce{HCO3-}} \cdot c_{\ce{H+}}}{c_{\ce{H2CO3^*}}}, \tag{6.7}\]

where \(c=\) molar or molal concentration, i.e., moles of species per L of water or moles per kg of water. At equilibrium, the numeric value of that reaction quotient, Equation 6.7, is know: at 15\(\degC\) it is \(3.80 \cdot 10^{-7}\) (Snoeyink (1980): p. 157). (Actually, except for dilute solutions, a correction is needed for interactions between ions, but the use of concentrations can still give decent approximate values. See Snoeyink (1980) for more information on chemical activity and ionic strength.) But typically, because the value of constants vary by orders of magnitude, it is the negative logarithm (base 10) and we’ll see \(pK_a\). So here, \(pK_a = 6.42\). The numeric value is called the equilibrium constant

So, if we know the concentration of \(\ce{H2CO3^*}\) and \(\ce{H^+}\) we can calculate the concentration of bicarbonate \(\ce{HCO3-}\) using the equilibrium constant. The hydrogen ion (or “proton”, because that it what it is!) concentration is commonly take from pH, i.e.,

\[ p\text{H} = - log_{10}(c_{H^+}). \tag{6.8}\]

Even better, if we could use other, independent, information, we can solve for all species concentrations, including \(\ce{H+}\), which means we could calculate pH. We can use element balance (commonly H balance) or charge balance for that other information. The programming might be a bit much for this course, so we will include an example in an appendix . . .

Here is a simple example for ammonia. This is another thing all environmental engineers should know!

The relevant proton exchange (acid-base) reaction is:

\[ \ce{NH4+ <=> NH3 + H+}, \tag{6.9}\]

with an equilbrium constant \(k_a\), where the \(a\) is for acid dissociation constant. The numeric value is around \(pk_a = 9.56\) at 15 \(\degC\) (Clegg and Whitfield (1995)).

Let’s write the mass action expression and set it to the acid dissociation constant:

\[ k_a = \frac{c_{\ce{NH3(aq)}} \cdot c_{\ce{H+}}} {c_{\ce{NH4+}}}. \tag{6.10}\]

What else can we use? Well, we cannot measure the ammonium (\(\ce{NH4+}\)) concentration directly. But it is trivial to measure the sum of \(\ce{NH4+}\) and \(\ce{NH3}\) as N mass, which is often called TAN (for total ammoniacal nitrogen) concentration in agriculture and related fields. And then from mass conservation, we know that:

\[ c_{\text{TAN}} = c_{\ce{NH4+}} + c_{\ce{NH3}(aq)}. \tag{6.11}\]

Lastly, we can estimate \(\ce{H+}\) from measured pH, or anyway use it as an input here. That’s all we need. To get an explicit closed-form solution, we can rearrange Equation 6.14 to solve for \(\ce{NH4+}\), and insert the result into Equation 6.10, and finally rearrange to solve for \(\ce{NH3(aq)}\)!

First, rearrange Equation 6.14 to solve for \(\ce{NH4+}\):

\[ c_{\ce{NH4+}} = c_{\text{TAN}} - c_{\ce{NH3}(aq)}. \tag{6.12}\]

And insert this into Equation 6.10:

\[ k_a = \frac{c_{\ce{NH3(aq)}} \cdot c_{\ce{H+}}} {c_{\text{TAN}} - c_{\ce{NH3}(aq)}}. \tag{6.13}\]

And rearrange:

\[ \frac{k_a} {c_{\ce{H+}}} \cdot (c_{\text{TAN}} - c_{\ce{NH3}(aq)}) = c_{\ce{NH3(aq)}} . \tag{6.14}\]
